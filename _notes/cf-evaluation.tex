\section{Fractional linear transformations}

A fractional linear transformation (or M\"obius transformation) is a function of the form
\begin{equation}
  f(z) = \frac{az + b}{cz + d},
\end{equation}
which we represent by the $2 \times 2$ matrix
\begin{equation}
  M = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
We write $M \cdot z$ for the action of $M$ on $z$ by the corresponding fractional linear transformation:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \cdot z = \frac{az + b}{cz + d}.
\end{equation}
The key property is that composition of fractional linear transformations corresponds to matrix multiplication: if $M_1$ and $M_2$ are two such matrices, then
\begin{equation}
  M_1 \cdot (M_2 \cdot z) = (M_1 M_2) \cdot z.
\end{equation}
Because matrix multiplication is associative, this gives us freedom to group and reorder the computation of a composition of fractional linear transformations in any way we like.

This action can be realized concretely by identifying a scalar $z$ with the homogeneous column vector $\binom{z}{1}$ and letting $M$ act by matrix-vector multiplication on the right:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} z \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} az + b \\ cz + d \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
which represents the value $(az + b)/(cz + d)$. The distinguished points correspond to
\begin{equation}
  0 \;\longleftrightarrow\; \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad
  \infty \;\longleftrightarrow\; \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
so acting on $\infty$ reads off the first column of the matrix ($a/c$), and acting on $0$ reads off the second column ($b/d$).

\section{Polynomials}

Consider a polynomial
\begin{equation}
  p(x) = a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \cdots + a_n x^n.
\end{equation}
Horner's method rewrites this as a nested evaluation
\begin{equation}
  p(x) = a_0 + x\bigl(a_1 + x\bigl(a_2 + \cdots + x \cdot a_n \cdots\bigr)\bigr).
\end{equation}
Each level of the nesting corresponds to a fractional linear transformation $z \mapsto a_k + xz$, represented by the matrix
\begin{equation}
  L_k = \begin{pmatrix} x & a_k \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The value of the polynomial is then given by acting on $\binom{0}{1}$ on the right:
\begin{equation}
  (L_0 L_1 \cdots L_n) \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} p(x) \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Writing
\begin{equation}
  L_0 L_1 \cdots L_k = \begin{pmatrix} x^{k+1} & p_k(x) \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
where $p_k(x) = a_0 + a_1 x + \cdots + a_k x^k$, we see that the accumulated product directly tracks the partial sums of the polynomial.

Because each $L_k$ has bottom row $(0, 1)$, the denominator of the homogeneous result is always $1$: there is no need to divide at the end. We can extract the polynomial value as a single number by also acting with a row vector on the left:
\begin{equation}
  p(x) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} L_0 L_1 \cdots L_n \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
This sandwiched form is purely a product of matrices and vectors, with no homogeneous-to-scalar conversion needed. Associativity now applies to the entire expression: we may group and partition the product in any way we like, and each sub-product is an ordinary matrix or scalar multiplication.

Accumulating the matrix product from right to left gives the \emph{backward recurrence}
\begin{equation}
  p(x) = a_0 + x\bigl(a_1 + x\bigl(a_2 + \cdots + x \cdot a_n\bigr)\cdots\bigr),
\end{equation}
which is the well-known Horner's method (or nested multiplication). Accumulating from left to right gives the \emph{forward recurrence}
\begin{equation}
  p(x) = \bigl(\cdots\bigl((a_n \cdot x + a_{n-1}) \cdot x + a_{n-2}\bigr) \cdot x + \cdots\bigr) \cdot x + a_0,
\end{equation}
which processes the coefficients in the opposite order, starting from the leading term. Because matrix multiplication is associative, both orderings---and any other grouping of the matrix product---yield the same result.

\subsection*{Estrin's method}

Beyond left-to-right and right-to-left accumulation, we can also group the matrix product into pairs and multiply in a balanced binary tree. Pairing adjacent matrices gives
\begin{equation}
  L_k L_{k+1}
  = \begin{pmatrix} x & a_k \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix} x & a_{k+1} \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}
  = \begin{pmatrix} x^2 & a_k + a_{k+1} x \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The nontrivial content of each pair product is just the linear combination $a_k + a_{k+1} x$, plus the power $x^2$ which is shared across all pairs. All pairs can be computed simultaneously. At the next level, each paired product has the form $\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} x^2 & q_j \\ 0 & 1 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$, so multiplying two of them gives $\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} x^4 & q_j + x^2 q_{j+1} \\ 0 & 1 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$: combine pairs using $x^2$ and compute $x^4$ for the next round.

This is exactly Estrin's method, which groups a polynomial into linear pieces that can be evaluated in parallel and then combines them level by level. The correspondence works cleanly because the bottom row stays $(0, 1)$ at every level---upper triangular times upper triangular is upper triangular---so the matrix product never does any work beyond what the scalar Estrin scheme does.

\subsection*{Rounding error}

In exact arithmetic, the forward and backward recurrences give identical results. In floating point, they differ slightly. Horner's method (backward recurrence) uses $n$ multiplications and $n$ additions, while the forward recurrence uses $2n$ multiplications and $n$ additions---the extra $n$ multiplications maintain the running power $x^k$. This leads to error bounds of roughly
\begin{equation}
  |\hat p - p(x)| \lesssim n\epsilon\,\tilde p(|x|) \quad\text{(backward)}, \qquad
  |\hat p - p(x)| \lesssim 2n\epsilon\,\tilde p(|x|) \quad\text{(forward)},
\end{equation}
where $\tilde p(|x|) = \sum_k |a_k|\,|x|^k$ and $\epsilon$ is machine epsilon. Both methods are backward stable: the computed result is the exact evaluation of a polynomial with slightly perturbed coefficients. The difference is just a constant factor.

The relative error for both methods is controlled by the condition number $\tilde p(|x|)/|p(x)|$, which measures the cancellation between terms. When all terms have the same sign---for instance, when $x > 0$ and all $a_k > 0$---the condition number is~$1$ and both methods are equally accurate. Large relative errors require cancellation between terms, regardless of the method used.

The situation is very different for other bases. In a three-term recurrence like the Chebyshev recurrence $T_{n+1}(x) = 2x\,T_n(x) - T_{n-1}(x)$, the recurrence is second order and has two linearly independent solutions. For $|x| > 1$, one solution grows exponentially and the other decays. Forward recurrence is genuinely unstable in this setting: roundoff error excites the growing parasitic solution, producing errors that grow exponentially with~$n$. Backward recurrence (Clenshaw's algorithm, or more generally Olver's algorithm) avoids this by naturally selecting the minimal solution. The monomial-basis recurrence is effectively first order, so there is no parasitic solution to excite, and the stability difference between forward and backward evaluation remains modest.

\section{Continued fractions}

Now consider a continued fraction
\begin{equation}
  a_0 + \cfrac{b_1}{a_1 + \cfrac{b_2}{a_2 + \cfrac{b_3}{a_3 + \cdots}}}.
\end{equation}
Each level of the nesting corresponds to a fractional linear transformation $z \mapsto a_n + b_{n+1}/z$, represented by the matrix
\begin{equation}
  L_n = \begin{pmatrix} a_n & b_{n+1} \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The $n$th convergent of the continued fraction is then given by acting on $\binom{1}{0}$ on the right:
\begin{equation}
  (L_0 L_1 \cdots L_n) \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} p_n \\ q_n \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
which represents the value $p_n/q_n$. Writing
\begin{equation}
  L_0 L_1 \cdots L_n = \begin{pmatrix} p_n & p_n' \\ q_n & q_n' \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
the columns of the accumulated product have a direct interpretation: $p_n/q_n$ is the $n$th convergent, and $p_n'/q_n'$ is the value obtained by truncating the continued fraction one step earlier (i.e.\ by setting $b_{n+1} = 0$ instead of evaluating $\cdot\,\infty$).

For the common case of a simple continued fraction where all $b_n = 1$, each matrix takes the form
\begin{equation}
  L_n = \begin{pmatrix} a_n & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}

The parallel with polynomial evaluation is exact: both reduce to computing a product of $2 \times 2$ matrices and then extracting the result by acting on a distinguished point ($0$ for polynomials, $\infty$ for continued fractions). Associativity of matrix multiplication gives the same freedom to reorganize the computation in either case.

One difference is worth noting. In the polynomial case, the maps $z \mapsto a_k + xz$ are affine: every $L_k$ has bottom row $(0, 1)$, so the accumulated product is upper triangular and the denominator is always~$1$. This is what allows the sandwiched form $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} L_0 \cdots L_n \binom{0}{1} = p(x)$ to yield a single number. In the continued fraction case, the maps $z \mapsto a_n + b_{n+1}/z$ are genuinely projective: the denominator $q_n$ is nontrivial, so the result is inherently a ratio $p_n / q_n$ and no choice of row vector on the left can collapse it to a scalar.
